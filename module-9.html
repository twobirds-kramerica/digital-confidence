Deepfake Videos and Audio

AI can create realistic fake videos of real people saying things they never actually said. These are called deepfakes.

Watch Out

Deepfakes are often used for: political misinformation, celebrity scams pretending a famous person recommends an investment, and â€” most dangerously â€” impersonating family members to ask for money.

How to spot a deepfake video:

  • Unnatural or limited blinking â€” real people blink naturally and often
  • Lip sync slightly off â€” the mouth does not quite match the words
  • Weird lighting on the face compared to the background
  • Slightly blurry edges around the face or hairline
  • The person seems stiff or robotic compared to how they normally move

AI-Generated Scam Emails

Scammers now use AI to write more convincing phishing emails. The grammar is better, the tone sounds more human, and the spelling is correct. But the red flags remain the same:

  • Urgency â€” "Act immediately or your account will be closed"
  • Requests for money, gift cards, or personal information
  • Unknown or suspicious sender address
  • Links that do not match the company they claim to be from

Apply the same security rules you learned in Module 2: The Security Shield. AI makes scam emails look better, but it cannot change their fundamental tactics.

Fake AI-Generated Photos

AI can create realistic photos of events that never happened â€” politicians "saying" things they never said, fake product reviews with AI-generated happy customers, and news stories that are entirely fabricated.

Quick Check

If a photo seems shocking or unbelievable, ask: "Does this appear on any reputable news sites like CBC, CTV, or the Toronto Star?" If only unknown websites are sharing it, be very skeptical.

ğŸ”’ Privacy Concerns with AI Tools

What Not to Share with AI Chatbots

Protect Your Privacy

When using AI chat tools like ChatGPT, never share: your Social Insurance Number, banking information, passwords, home address, medical details, or any other personal information. Your conversations may be saved and used to improve the AI system.

Safe to share with AI: General questions, recipe ideas, how-to instructions, help writing a thank-you note, questions about history or science. Think of it like asking a knowledgeable stranger at a library â€” helpful for general questions, but no personal details.

Companies May Save Your Data

Free AI services often keep your questions and answers. Photos you upload might be stored on their servers. When in doubt, look for "privacy mode" or "incognito" settings in AI tools, or choose Canadian-based services that follow PIPEDA privacy law.

Good News

Your iPad or iPhone's built-in AI features â€” like photo enhancement and Face ID â€” process everything on your device only. Apple does not see your photos or your face. That data never leaves your device.
