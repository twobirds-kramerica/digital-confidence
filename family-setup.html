Setting This Up for Someone You Care About

If you are here because you want to help a parent, grandparent, or friend get started with the Digital Confidence Centre — thank you. This page walks you through a 10-minute setup so they can start learning right away, even if you are not in the room with them. You do not need to be a tech expert. You just need a few minutes and a little patience.

Everything on this site is free, self-paced, and designed specifically for people who are new to — or nervous about — technology. The modules are written in plain language, with no jargon, no pressure, and plenty of encouragement built in.

💡 You are in the right place. This page is written for you — the helper — not for the senior learner. The tone here is peer-to-peer. We assume you can handle a little more technical context.

Step 1 — Choose Their City

The Digital Confidence Centre personalises local support resources based on where your loved one lives. When their city is set, the Resources page will automatically show them their nearest library tech help programme, local senior centre, and Apple Store — as well as national programmes like Connected Canadians.

This setting is stored only on their device (in the browser's local storage). It is never sent to any server. If they use the site on a different device, you would set it up there too.

Click the button below to open the setup wizard, then choose their city on the first screen:

💡 Cities currently supported: Windsor, London, Kitchener, St. Thomas, and Woodstock. More coming soon. If their city is not listed, choose the nearest one — the national resources will still be fully accurate.

Step 2 — Tell the Site Which Devices They Use

After choosing their city, the wizard will ask about their devices — phone, tablet, and computer. This allows the site to show only the instructions that apply to their actual devices. If they have an iPad, they will see iPad steps. If they do not have a Windows computer, those sections are hidden automatically.

The same setup wizard (button above) handles both city and devices in a single flow. Work through it together if you can — it only takes about two minutes.

💡 Not sure what device they have? Check the back of the device for the brand name. iPhone/iPad = Apple. Samsung, Google Pixel, or most others = Android. Windows laptop = Windows. MacBook = Mac.

Step 3 — Add the Site to Their Home Screen

Adding the Digital Confidence Centre to their home screen means they can open it with a single tap — just like any other app. It will look and feel like an installed app, even though it is just a website. This removes the barrier of having to remember a web address or find it through a browser.

On iPhone or iPad (using Safari)

  1. Open Safari (the blue compass icon) and go to the site. The address is: twobirds-kramerica.github.io/digital-confidence
  2. Tap the Share button — it looks like a square with an arrow pointing up, at the bottom of the screen (iPhone) or top of the screen (iPad).
    📤 Image needed: Safari Share button on iPhone — square with upward arrow at the bottom toolbar
  3. Scroll down in the menu that appears and tap "Add to Home Screen".
  4. Tap "Add" in the top right corner. Done.

The icon will now appear on their home screen. Tapping it opens the site in full-screen mode, with no browser address bar.

On Android (using Chrome)

  1. Open Chrome (the multicoloured circle icon) and go to the site.
  2. Tap the three dots menu in the top right corner of the screen.
    ⋮ Image needed: Chrome three-dot menu icon in the top right corner of the browser
  3. Tap "Add to Home Screen" from the menu.
  4. Tap "Add" to confirm. Done.

Step 4 — Start Them on Module 1

The best starting point is Module 1: Mastering the Escape Hatch. It teaches the single most important skill on any device — how to get back to safety, no matter what has appeared on the screen. Once they have this skill, everything else feels much less frightening.

You can either sit with them for Module 1, or set it up and let them go at their own pace. The module takes about 20–30 minutes and includes several exercises.

Step 5 — How to Check In

You do not need to be present for every module — the site is designed for independent, self-paced learning. But a little check-in goes a long way.

How do you know they are making progress? Each module has a progress checklist at the bottom — small checkboxes they can tick as they complete each step. These are saved automatically on their device. If you visit them in person, you can look at any module page together and see which boxes are ticked.

A simple approach that works well: after each module, send a text or give a quick call to ask how it went. "Did you try the fingerprint thing?" or "Did you figure out how to block a number?" These conversations reinforce what they learned and make them feel seen and proud of their progress.

💡 Most seniors learn best when they know someone cares about their progress. You do not need to explain anything yourself — the modules do that. You just need to ask how it went.

Step 6 — If They Get Stuck

These two free services are staffed by real, patient people who specialise in helping older adults with technology. Both are available by phone — no app, no video call required (unless they want it).

💡 Connected Canadians — free, one-on-one tech help by phone.
1-855-808-0505
Funded by the Government of Canada. Over 10,000 seniors served. Available Monday–Friday, 9am–5pm ET.
💡 Cyber-Seniors — free tech help from trained senior volunteers.
1-844-217-3057
Award-winning non-profit. Senior volunteers helping other seniors — in plain language, at your pace.
